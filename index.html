Writing

Wineskins

Last night, over almond milk and polvorones, my grandmother resurfaced the jug conversation for the fourth time this month.
The porcelain milk jug was a present she gave my grandpa for his 32nd birthday, in 1965, that read ‘The one who loves you will make you weep”. She mentioned it again in 2020 to justify why she’d just accused him of being an alcoholic for pouring a dollop of anís into his milk. My grandma Concha always storms off when she drops a verbal bomb, as if sweeping the entryway and then offering us another polvorón will magically restart the evening. As soon as she’s gone, grandad chuckles. Antonio is his name. He looks down at the deck of cards, la baraja española, and starts shuffling them.
“I thought old men didn’t have to take this anymore,” he muttered to himself. “When I was a kid, I’d hear: ‘what a shame it is that you’re eating the bread that could feed the goats’. One time, I was grazing the sheep and a boy my age came to talk to me. We were eight at the time. And my dad ran over to me and screamed ‘if you don’t focus I will rip your skin off to make botas de vino, wineskins.’ He hit me so hard I fell onto a newly pruned vine spur and started bleeding. I thought I was going to faint.”
I stared at him, inarticulate. His wrinkled eyes met mine, “They said they loved me, too. Is that love? ‘Cause if it is, I’d rather be despised.”
